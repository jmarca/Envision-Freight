Fact Sheets: Corridor Protection and Preservation

For a table of corridor preservation resource materials please click here.

Much of the research conducted on corridor protection and preservation over the past twenty-five years surrounding notes that prior planning for and around corridors (including the mapping of corridors) is a critical element to ensure continued corridor functionality and viability. Local comprehensive plans, MPO long-range plans and state freight plan development processes are the main logical places in which planning should start the process to create a preservation focus. Regional planning and visioning processes are another excellent venue to create a forum to plan for effective corridor protection that also considers freight corridors and major freight facilities.

Because the possibility that many of the conflicts between freight and other land uses are could be repeated a statewide basis, some states have chosen to take a legislative approach to reduce conflicts that arise because of incompatible land uses adjacent to freight corridors. Although the legislative process can be time consuming and unpredictable, having a state-wide policy to guide future land use decisions around corridors can save time, create stability in the long run and lead to more effective corridor preservation and protection.  

In some states, advance planning and approval of transportation corridors does not require a change in statutes or regulation.  However, corridor approval will require environmental analyses under the National Environmental Policy Act 1969 (NEPA) to determine and confirm the final corridor location on a map if federal funds are being utilized.   

Such advanced planning when more land is vacant allows local governments and private parties to better plan developments.  This helps minimize social, economic, and environmental impacts, including the impacts that arise from proximity of potentially incompatible land uses.  Advanced planning is also an excellent preservation tool that can be used in the freight context, because it recognizes the importance of the corridor (and any freight facilities associated with such a corridor).  It provides an excellent mechanism for local jurisdictions to effectively utilize their zoning powers to protect and preserve facilities from development uses that may be incompatible. Advanced planning also provides notice to citizens, property owners, and developers through adoption of an official thoroughfare protection map. 

The most often cited examples of corridor management legislation include Florida’s 1995 corridor management legislation and North Carolina’s rail corridor management legislation. 

NORTH CAROLINA: Rail Corridor Management Legislation

Conflict/Barrier

Policy

Result

State observed an emerging pattern of abandonment of railroad corridors; became concerned that the loss of these critical linear corridors could harm economic development efforts because of the difficulty in reassembling them for future rail transportation purposes.

Statute restricts development of property adjacent to the corridor:

Residential development along a corridor shall not interfere with the ultimate purpose of the corridor. Commercial and industrial development along a preserved corridor shall not adversely impact the corridor.

Statute restricts interim use of corridor to uses compatible with railroad development.

 A city or county that acquires property to preserve a railroad corridor may lease the property or use the property for interim compatible uses until the property is used for a railroad. 

NCGSA §§ 160A-498.

Local jurisdictions and the freight railroads with State’s assistance have started creating partnerships to manage interim uses pending re-start of freight services.  State has greater understanding of freight rail’s business needs which translates into a successful rail policy.

 

FLORIDA: Corridor Management Legislation

Conflict/Barrier

Policy

Result

State desired to designate future transportation corridors and hold them for development.  Corridor management legislation adopted requiring corridor mapping.

Local governments created corridor planning mapping with a five-year horizon and withheld development permits for five years. This blanket moratorium on development could be extended to ten years.

Florida Supreme Court held right-of-way preservation measures were un-constitutional takings. Court took issue with the automatic nature of the development freeze and statute’s stated purpose of controlling land values in anticipation of condemnation.  Joint Ventures v. Florida Department of Transportation, 563 So. 2d 625, 626 (Fla. 1990). 

Using corridor management legislation discussed above, Palm Beach County developed a different approach to local issues.

Palm Beach County developed a thoroughfare plan map which limited development to the extent necessary to ensure compatibility with future land uses.

Florida Supreme Court upheld the county’s thoroughfare plan map: (1) plans helped ensure adequate transportation facilities; (2) county’s map mandated by state legislation; (3) efforts represented invaluable planning tools that provided for future growth in accordance with state statutory objectives; (4) ability of local governments to amend their plans twice a year provided flexibility to mitigate hardships.

Palm Beach County v. Wright, 641 So. 2d 50, 53-54 (Fla. 1994).

In response to decisions, Florida amended state planning law in 1995.

New legislation expanded role of local governments in corridor management, while increasing coordination between local and state planning efforts. Instead of strictly limiting development, amendments focus on providing for compatible use “…within and adjacent to…corridor[s] to promote orderly growth” (Florida Statutes §163.3164).

Demonstrates the careful balance that must be found between appropriate state-coordinated corridor management and unlawful government takings.
